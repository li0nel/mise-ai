Before we start, three things Julia was emphatic about:

🥩
Dry the beef
Pat every piece of beef completely dry with kitchen paper before browning. Wet beef steams instead of browning — and browning is where 40% of the flavour lives. Julia used to get frustrated watching people skip this.
🍷
Use good wine
Julia's rule: if you wouldn't drink it, don't cook with it. The wine reduces and concentrates — a bad wine becomes a very bad sauce. A decent £10-12 bottle is all you need.
🔥
Brown in small batches
Never crowd the pan. The beef must sear, not steam. Work in batches of 4–5 pieces maximum, with hot fat and space between them. A packed pan drops the temperature and ruins the Maillard reaction.

Cook Now 6 steps
1
Blanch the lardons

Bring a medium pot of water to a boil. Add 170g lardons and blanch for 8 minutes. This removes excess salt and the "smoky" flavour so it doesn't overwhelm the finished dish. Drain and pat dry with kitchen paper.

⏱ 8 min blanch
2
Render the lardons

In a large heavy casserole (Dutch oven), heat 1 tbsp butter + 1 tbsp oil over medium heat. Add the lardons and render until golden and lightly crisp, about 3–4 minutes. Remove to a side bowl with a slotted spoon — leave the fat in the pan. You'll use this flavour-rich fat for the beef.

⏱ 3–4 min render
3
Brown the beef in batches

Pat every piece of 1.3kg beef completely dry with kitchen paper. Season generously with salt and pepper. Raise heat to high. In batches of 4–5 pieces (never crowding the pan), sear beef on all sides until deeply browned — about ⏱ 3 min per batch. Set aside with the lardons. The fond forming on the pan bottom is liquid gold.

⚠️ Crowded pan = steamed beef = grey, sad, flavourless. Space between pieces is mandatory. Work in batches. The whole process takes 15–20 minutes but it cannot be rushed.
4
Deglaze the pot

Reduce heat to medium. Add the sliced carrots and onion to the pot. Cook 5 minutes until softened. Add 4 cloves garlic and cook 1 minute. Sprinkle 3 tbsp flour over everything and stir to coat. Now pour in half the bottle of wine and scrape vigorously with a wooden spoon, releasing every bit of fond. This is the deglaze — watch the pot transform instantly.

5
Braise in the oven

Return the browned beef and lardons to the pot. Add the remaining wine, 500ml beef stock, 2 tbsp tomato paste, and the bouquet garni. The liquid should come about ¾ up the beef. Bring to a bare simmer on the stovetop, then cover and place in the oven at 160°C / 325°F.

⏱ 2½–3 hours in oven
💡 Julia's test: the beef should be easily pierced with a fork with no resistance. Not falling apart — that's overcooked. Tender but intact.
6
Sauté the garnitures

Julia's garnitures are cooked separately and added near the end. In a small pan, sauté 18–24 pearl onions in 1 tbsp butter over medium heat until golden, about 8–10 minutes. In the same pan, sauté 450g quartered mushrooms in 1 tbsp butter over high heat until browned, about 5 minutes. Stir both into the casserole during the last 30 minutes of braising.

⏱ Add 30 min before end
— 2 hours later — checking back in —
It's been 2 hours. Smells incredible. Beef is very tender but the sauce seems thin — almost watery. Is that normal?

Completely normal at this stage! The sauce will thin as it cooks because the beef releases moisture. You reduce it at the end — this is actually exactly how Julia intended it. Here's the finish:

🍷
Sauce Recovery — Reduction Finish
1
Remove beef with a slotted spoon and keep warm in a covered bowl. Leave sauce in the pot over the stove.
2
Bring to a rapid boil uncovered for 12–15 minutes until reduced by about ⅓ and it coats the back of a spoon. Skim off any fat that rises.
3
Taste and adjust with salt, a few grinds of pepper, and if you like, a small splash of the remaining wine for brightness.
4
Finish with cold butter — the monter au beurre: cut 2 tbsp cold butter into cubes, take the pan off heat, and whisk them in one at a time until glossy and thickened. Return beef to sauce.

This reduction step is actually how Julia specified the finish — many home cooks miss it because they expect the sauce to be perfect straight from the oven. The monter au beurre is a French technique (literally "butter mounting") where cold butter is whisked into a hot sauce off heat, adding gloss, body and a rounded richness that nothing else can replicate.

The sauce is absolutely gorgeous now. Rich, glossy, incredible. How do I serve?

You've made something exceptional — serve it properly. Three classic options, each correct in its own way:

🥔
Buttery mashed potato
Julia's choice. The richness of the pommes purée is the perfect foil for the wine-dark sauce. Make it obscenely buttery — this is not the time for restraint.
🍝
Buttered egg noodles
The classic bistro presentation. Wide pappardelle or egg noodles tossed in butter, with the bourguignon spooned over. The noodles soak up the sauce beautifully.
🥖
Crusty bread only
If your bread is exceptional, sometimes less is more. Tear it into the sauce. Drink the wine you cooked with.
